## 题目
A smart photovoltaic power station adopts 6 groups of periodic irradiance sensors to monitor the real-time light intensity distribution of the panel array. To achieve precise inverter efficiency regulation, the system calculates a **cube root comprehensive index of irradiance**. The instantaneous signal intensity (in \( \text{kW/m}^2 \)) of the \( k \)-th group of sensors is defined as \( 4 - |3\cos(k\pi t) - 2| \), where \( t \) is the **normalized time** within a day (\( t \in [0,1] \); \( t=0 \) corresponds to midnight 00:00, and \( t=1 \) corresponds to midnight 00:00 of the next day).

The calculation formula for the cube root comprehensive index of irradiance is:
$$w = \sqrt[3]{\left(4 - |3\cos(\pi t) - 2|\right) \cdot \left(4 - |3\cos(2\pi t) - 2|\right) \cdot \dots \cdot \left(4 - |3\cos(6\pi t) - 2|\right)}$$

The inverter enters the high-efficiency working mode **only when this index is greater than 3** (i.e., \( w > 3 \)). Find the number of disjoint open intervals corresponding to the high-efficiency working periods of the inverter in the normalized time interval \( [0,1] \) of a day.

## 解答
1. Transform the Cube Root Inequality to an Equivalent Algebraic Inequality
The inverter enters high-efficiency mode if and only if \( w > 3 \). Since the cube root function \( \sqrt[3]{x} \) is **strictly increasing** on \( \mathbb{R} \), the inequality:
$$\sqrt[3]{\prod_{k=1}^6 \left(4 - |3\cos(k\pi t) - 2|\right)} > 3$$
is equivalent to:
$$H(t) = \prod_{k=1}^6 \left(4 - |3\cos(k\pi t) - 2|\right) > 3^3 = 27.$$
Our goal is to find the number of disjoint open intervals \( t \in (0,1) \) that satisfy \( H(t) > 27 \).

2. Derive the Necessary and Sufficient Precondition for \( H(t) > 27 \)
First, analyze the range of a single factor \( f_k(t) = 4 - |3\cos(k\pi t) - 2| \) for \( k = 1,2,\dots,6 \):
- For any \( t \in (0,1) \), \( \cos(k\pi t) \in [-1, 1] \), so \( 3\cos(k\pi t) - 2 \in [-5, 1] \).
- Taking the absolute value: \( |3\cos(k\pi t) - 2| \in [0, 5] \), thus \( f_k(t) \in [-1, 4] \).

Since \( H(t) > 27 > 0 \), the product of 6 factors must be positive. This gives two cases:
1. **All \( f_k(t) > 0 \)**: The maximum product of 6 positive factors is \( 4^6 = 4096 \gg 27 \), so this case is feasible.
2. **All \( f_k(t) < 0 \)**: The maximum product of 6 negative factors is \( (-1)^6 = 1 < 27 \), so this case is impossible.

Thus, the **necessary and sufficient precondition** for \( H(t) > 27 \) is:
1. All \( f_k(t) > 0 \) (i.e., \( |3\cos(k\pi t) - 2| < 4 \)).
2. \( \prod_{k=1}^6 f_k(t) > 27 \).

Simplify the first condition:
$$|3\cos(k\pi t) - 2| < 4 \iff -4 < 3\cos(k\pi t) - 2 < 4.$$
- The right inequality \( 3\cos(k\pi t) < 6 \iff \cos(k\pi t) < 2 \) is **always true** (since \( \cos\theta \leq 1 \)).
- The left inequality \( 3\cos(k\pi t) > -2 \iff \cos(k\pi t) > -\frac{2}{3} \).

We only need to analyze intervals where \( \cos(k\pi t) > -\frac{2}{3} \) for all \( k = 1,2,\dots,6 \), and \( H(t) > 27 \) in these intervals.

3. Piecewise Expression and Monotonicity of \( f_k(t) \)
The absolute value in \( f_k(t) \) introduces a piecewise structure. The segmentation point is where \( 3\cos(k\pi t) - 2 = 0 \), i.e., \( \cos(k\pi t) = \frac{2}{3} \). For \( \cos(k\pi t) > -\frac{2}{3} \) (our precondition), \( f_k(t) \) has two piecewise forms:
1. **When \( \cos(k\pi t) \geq \frac{2}{3} \)**: \( 3\cos(k\pi t) - 2 \geq 0 \), so \( f_k(t) = 4 - (3\cos(k\pi t) - 2) = 6 - 3\cos(k\pi t) \).
2. **When \( -\frac{2}{3} < \cos(k\pi t) < \frac{2}{3} \)**: \( 3\cos(k\pi t) - 2 < 0 \), so \( f_k(t) = 4 - (2 - 3\cos(k\pi t)) = 2 + 3\cos(k\pi t) \).

4. Key Nodes and Monotonicity of \( H(t) \)
Key nodes are the solutions to \( \cos(k\pi t) = \frac{2}{3} \) or \( \cos(k\pi t) = -\frac{2}{3} \) for \( k = 1,2,\dots,6 \) and \( t \in (0,1) \). These nodes divide \( (0,1) \) into disjoint open intervals. In each interval:
- For all \( k \), the sign of \( 3\cos(k\pi t) - 2 \) is constant (so \( f_k(t) \) has a fixed piecewise form).
- \( \cos(k\pi t) \) is strictly monotonic (since no solutions to \( \cos(k\pi t) = c \) exist in the interval).
- Thus, each \( f_k(t) \) is strictly monotonic, and their product \( H(t) \) is **strictly monotonic** in each open interval.

5. Evaluate \( H(t) \) at Critical Points and Analyze Interval Behavior
1. **As \( t \to 0^+ \)**:
   \( \cos(k\pi t) \to 1 \) for all \( k \). Since \( 1 \geq \frac{2}{3} \), \( f_k(t) \to 6 - 3 \times 1 = 3 \). Thus:
   $$H(t) \to 3^6 = 729 > 27.$$
   The interval adjacent to \( t=0 \) is effective (at least partially).

2. **As \( t \) increases from 0**:
   The first key node \( t_1 \) is the **smallest solution** to \( \cos(k\pi t) = \frac{2}{3} \) (for \( k=6 \), since larger \( k \) makes \( k\pi t \) increase faster). In \( (0, t_1) \), all \( \cos(k\pi t) > \frac{2}{3} \), so \( f_k(t) = 6 - 3\cos(k\pi t) \). As \( t \) increases:
   - \( \cos(k\pi t) \) strictly decreases (since \( k\pi t \in (0, \arccos(\frac{2}{3})) \subset (0, \frac{\pi}{2}) \)).
   - Thus, \( f_k(t) = 6 - 3\cos(k\pi t) \) **strictly increases** (decreasing \( \cos\theta \) leads to increasing \( 6 - 3\cos\theta \)).
   - All \( f_k(t) \) are strictly increasing, so \( H(t) \) is **strictly increasing** in \( (0, t_1) \).

3. **As \( t \) increases beyond \( t_1 \)**:
   For \( t > t_1 \), some \( f_k(t) \) switch to the form \( 2 + 3\cos(k\pi t) \), which strictly decreases as \( t \) increases. As \( t \) continues to increase, more \( f_k(t) \) become decreasing, and \( H(t) \) starts to **strictly decrease** overall.

4. **At the precondition boundary \( t^* \)**:
   \( t^* \) is the smallest solution to \( \cos(k\pi t) = -\frac{2}{3} \). At \( t = t^* \), one \( f_k(t) = 0 \), so \( H(t^*) = 0 < 27 \). For \( t > t^* \), \( \cos(k\pi t) < -\frac{2}{3} \) for some \( k \), so \( f_k(t) < 0 \) and \( H(t) < 0 < 27 \).

5. **For \( t \in (\frac{1}{2}, 1) \)**:
   For odd \( k \) (e.g., \( k=1,3,5 \)), \( k\pi t \in (\frac{k\pi}{2}, k\pi) \). As \( t \) increases beyond \( \frac{1}{2} \), \( \cos(k\pi t) \) decreases to -1, which is less than \( -\frac{2}{3} \). This violates the precondition, so \( H(t) < 0 < 27 \). No effective intervals exist here.

6. Determine Effective Intervals Using Continuity and Monotonicity
- \( H(t) \) is continuous on \( (0, t^*) \) (product of continuous functions).
- \( H(t) \to 729 > 27 \) as \( t \to 0^+ \), and \( H(t^*) = 0 < 27 \).
- Since \( H(t) \) is strictly increasing in \( (0, t_1) \) and strictly decreasing for \( t > t_1 \), there exists a **unique \( t_0 \in (0, t^*) \)** such that \( H(t_0) = 27 \).
- For \( t \in (0, t_0) \), \( H(t) > 27 \) (effective). For \( t \in (t_0, t^*) \), \( H(t) < 27 \) (ineffective). For \( t \geq t^* \), \( H(t) \leq 0 < 27 \) (ineffective).

### 7. Final Conclusion
The only effective working period of the inverter is the open interval \( (0, t_0) \). Thus, the number of disjoint open intervals corresponding to the high-efficiency working periods is \(\boldsymbol{1}\).

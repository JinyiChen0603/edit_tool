## 题目
In a crystallography study, a molecule is modeled as a quadrangular pyramid $P\!-\!ABCD$.  
The base atoms form a rhombus $ABCD$ with side length $2$ angstroms and 
\[
\angle ABC = 120^\circ .
\]
The apical atom $P$ is positioned such that the bond $PA$ is perpendicular to the base plane $ABCD$.  
Let $E$ be the midpoint of the bond $BC$ and $F$ the midpoint of the bond $PC$.  

An incoming particle $H$ travels along the line $PD$. When the angle between the trajectory $EH$ and the plane $PAD$ reaches its maximum value, the tangent of this angle is measured as $\sqrt{2}$.  

Determine the cosine of the dihedral angle $E\!-\!AF\!-\!C$ between the planes defined by $\triangle AEF$ and $\triangle AFC$.


## 解答
\textbf{Solution}

\textbf{Step 1: Geometric relations}

Since $ABCD$ is a rhombus with $\angle ABC = 120^\circ$, triangle $ABC$ is an obtuse isosceles triangle.  
The length of diagonal $AC$ is
\[
AC=\sqrt{2^2+2^2-2\cdot2\cdot2\cos120^\circ}
=\sqrt{12}=2\sqrt{3}.
\]
Because $E$ is the midpoint of $BC$, we have
\[
AE=\frac{AC}{2}=\sqrt{3}.
\]
As before, $AE\perp BC$, and since $BC\parallel AD$, we obtain $AE\perp AD$.  
Together with $PA\perp$ plane $ABCD$, it follows that
\[
AE\perp \text{plane } PAD.
\]

Let $\theta$ be the angle between $EH$ and plane $PAD$. Then $\theta=\angle EHA$, and in right triangle $\triangle EAH$,
\[
\tan\theta=\frac{AE}{AH}.
\]
The maximum of $\theta$ occurs when $AH$ is minimal, i.e. when $AH\perp PD$.  
Given $\tan\theta=\sqrt{2}$ and $AE=\sqrt{3}$, we find
\[
\frac{\sqrt{3}}{AH}=\sqrt{2}
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
AH=\sqrt{\frac{3}{2}}.
\]

In right triangle $\triangle PAD$, $AD=2$ and $AH$ is the altitude to hypotenuse $PD$. Hence,
\[
PA^2 = AD^2 + AH^2 = 4 + \frac{3}{2} = \frac{11}{2},
\qquad
PA=\sqrt{\frac{11}{2}}.
\]

\textbf{Step 2: Coordinate system}

Take $A$ as the origin. Let $AE$, $AD$, and $AP$ be the positive $x$-, $y$-, and $z$-axes, respectively.  
Then
\[
A(0,0,0),\quad
B(\sqrt{3},-1,0),\quad
C(\sqrt{3},1,0),\quad
D(0,2,0),\quad
P\!\left(0,0,\sqrt{\tfrac{11}{2}}\right).
\]
Thus,
\[
E(\sqrt{3},0,0),\qquad
F\!\left(\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2},\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{\tfrac{11}{2}}\right).
\]

\textbf{Step 3: Dihedral angle}

For plane $AEF$, a normal vector $\vec n_1$ satisfies
\[
\vec n_1\cdot \vec{AE}=0,\qquad
\vec n_1\cdot \vec{AF}=0,
\]
which yields a normal vector
\[
\vec n_1=(0,\sqrt{11},-2).
\]

For plane $AFC$, note that diagonal $BD$ is perpendicular to both $AC$ and $PA$, hence $BD\perp$ plane $AFC$.  
Therefore, a normal vector of plane $AFC$ is
\[
\vec n_2=\vec{BD}=(-\sqrt{3},3,0).
\]

Let $\phi$ be the dihedral angle $E\!-\!AF\!-\!C$. Then
\[
\cos\phi
=\frac{|\vec n_1\cdot \vec n_2|}{|\vec n_1|\,|\vec n_2|}
=\frac{3\sqrt{11}}{\sqrt{11}\,\sqrt{12}}
=\frac{3}{2\sqrt{3}}
=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}.
\]

\bigskip

\textbf{Answer}

The cosine of the dihedral angle $E\!-\!AF\!-\!C$ is
\[
\boxed{\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}}.
\]

## 答案
\[
\boxed{\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}}.
\]
